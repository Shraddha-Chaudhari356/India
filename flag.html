About the National Flag

The national flag of India, also known as the Tiranga (meaning "Tricolour"), was adopted on 22 July 1947, shortly before India's independence from British rule.

It is a horizontal tricolour of:

  • Saffron at the top, symbolizing courage and sacrifice
  • White in the middle, representing peace and truth
  • Green at the bottom, standing for faith and chivalry

In the center of the white band is the Ashoka Chakra, a navy-blue 24-spoke wheel representing the eternal wheel of law (Dharma).

Respecting the Flag

According to the Flag Code of India, the national flag should be treated with dignity and should never touch the ground or be used for commercial purposes.

The Do's:

  • The National Flag may be hoisted in educational institutions (schools, colleges, sports camps, scout camps, etc.) to inspire respect for the Flag. An oath of allegiance has been included in the flag hoisting in schools.
  • A member of the public, a private organization, or an educational institution may hoist/display the National Flag on all days and occasions, ceremonial or otherwise, consistent with the dignity and honour of the National Flag.
  • Section 2 of the new code accepts the right of all private citizens to fly the flag on their premises.

The Don'ts:

  • The flag cannot be used for communal gains, drapery, or clothes. It should be flown from sunrise to sunset, irrespective of the weather.
  • The flag must not intentionally touch the ground, floor, or trail in water.
  • It cannot be draped over the hood, top, sides, or back of vehicles, trains, boats, or aircraft.
  • No other flag or bunting should be placed higher than the National Flag.
  • No objects such as flowers, garlands, or emblems should be placed on or above the flag.
  • The Tricolour cannot be used as a festoon, rosette, or bunting.
